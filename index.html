WHERE ARE YOU FROM?

Monday, February 1st, 2021
2:46pm ET

After reading Paul Chan's Letter to Young Artists during a Pandemic, I asked myself a simple question. Where am I from? I grew up in New York City and therefore have a very strong tie to this magical place. Like most who grew up in the city that never sleeps, I inherently think that it is superior over all places. However, as I began to ask myself this simple question, I started to think about the concept of home. On this snowy Monday, I am reminded of my family's snow day traditions and rituals. I realize that the sledding in the park, the hot chocolate with marshmallows, and the pancakes for breakfast are the true definitions of home. The relationship between the people, things, and memories that make a house feel like a home reminds me of the relationship between CSS and HTML. HTML is New York City: the bare bones of the home, the technicalities, the place. CSS is what makes a home unique with individualized memories and traditions: everyone's concept of home is styled differently.

ARCHIVING THE INTERNET

Monday, February 8th, 2021
3:48pm ET

Jill Lepore's Can the Internet be Archived forced me to think about Twitter's significant and historic role in the 2020 presidential election. Trump's excessive, devisive, and deranged Twitter usage was a huge part of the election's narrative. Without Trump's tweets, the story would have looked extremely different. What if in 10, 20, 30 or however many years, these tweets disappeared? How would future generations comprehend what occuredd? Would a historic view of the present look extremely different from its reality? I believe so. I believe that without tangible records of what Trump wrote in his tweets, future generations would not be able to fully comprehend his character - or lack there of. Luckily for future generations, Twitter has committed to have all tweets archived in The Libarary of Congress. But what if Twitter did not made this promise? And are we saving too much? How does our process of archiving the past, present, and future of information that is spread by technological means compare to how those that came before us - without the use of the internet - saved information for future generatons?

COUNTERCULTURE?

Tuesday, February 16th, 2021
8:18pm ET

Caroline Busta's The internet didn't kill counterculture - you just won't find it on Instagram was very thought provoking. Counterculture, according to Merriam-Webster, is defined as "a culture with values and mores that run counter to those of established society." Busta questioned whether or not counterculture truly exists in today's social media and internet driven society. The internet is a place that enables personal expression for all users. What does having power on the internet and/or social media really mean today? Is it having a verrified account? Is it having followers? Is it having a voice? Or is it what the user does with their voice? Busta believes that the power lies in the control of "the means by which lesser power can be displayed. Actual power owns social media." So perhaps no average internet or social media user actually has power. And if they users don't truly have power, can they really ever be countercultural?

THE GOOD ROOM

Tuesday, February 23rd, 2021
9:29pm ET

The Good Room by Frank Chimero stated: "In the last decade, technology has transformed from a tool that we use to a place where we live." After reading this quote, I paused and thought about this simple statement's complete and utter accuracy. We don't use technology as a device to solve problems, we use it as a means to exist, to evolve, to transform ourselves. Technology has become a persona, and the way that we use technology has become a narriatve. How do we shape the story so that we use technology for good? Or is it too late? Chimero suggests that since tehcnology has become a home, a place, an enviornment, we need "choose to be in lively and nourshing digital environments." This article forced me to examine the digital spaces in which I currently live becuase if I don't enjoy living in these spaces, why do I?

ON OR WITHIN THE DIGITAL?

Monday, March 1st, 2021
5:31pm ET

Jodi's Infrastructure by Alexander R. Galloway questioned whether one thinks of themselves as working "on" the digital or "within" the digital. Is the digital at the core of your work or is the digital just related to your work? I really thought about this question and couldn't come up with an answer. As a graphic designer, the computer is the way in which I complete my work - all of the programs that I use purely exist on the computer. However, is the ethos of the work that I create directly linked to my computer? Would the essence of the work change if the computer changed? Is the context of the computer becoming my content? Or is my content providing its own individual context, detached from the computer?

AGENCY?

Tuesday, March 9th, 2021
10:31am ET

Silvio Lorusso's The User Condition: Computer Agency and Behavior touches on ideas surrounding agency, labor, work, and action. The reading brought up the idea of the computer being a world within a world and questioned the agency that individuals have within and surrounding its digital ecosystem. I found Lorusso's piece very theoretical and philosphical and as a result, I did not completely grasp every aspect of the reading. What I did take away from the piece was that the computer questions the boundaries that the traditional definition of agency provides to individuals in a non-digital world. What does it truly mean to have agency in present day society? How can we combat the social pressures that both control and limit one's agency? Lorusso redefines agency as "the capacity for action, which is in turn the ability to interrupt behavior." But in a world where the digital is taking over every day actions, work, and labor, can agency truly exist?

CONTROL

Tuesday, March 30th, 2021
6:21pm ET

I immediately thought of the game of chess while reading Concept Design Games Book Two: Playing. According to the reading, control is the central concept "to all making, using, and designing of artifacts." Control sits in the place that enables us to choose the placement of things and the way in which they are placed. Control is therefore influenced by interaction: it is by interacting with others that we can exercise control over one another. In order to be a master chess player, one must be able to successfully move one's own pieces and predict the movement of one's opponent's pieces. This is all done in silence - like the Silent Game described in the reading. Action is the sole form of interaction and communication. Moreover, the game of chess demonstrates how control realy lies in the ability to take autonomy away from an opponent, or taking away control from others. Therefore, control is not only the ability to control oneself and one's decisions, but it is also the ability to control the decisions of others. The following quote particuarly stuck with me: "Control is part of all games; making a game baased on a concept is to express the concept in terms of control." If life is a big game, then our ability to survive and succeed in society is determined by the strength of our control over ourselves, our choices, and our interaction with others. What does that say about us?

TITLE

Tuesday, April 6th, 2021
12:49pm ET

Amanda K. Greene's Data Sweat describes the importance of "Digital Exhaust" - metadata that is generated by our online interactions and usage. Greene explains that it is not only the posts that we as indivduals hand select to share with the world that provide information. It is also the behind-the-scenes interactions between humans and technology that tell a story. The ways in which we interact with our devices and online platforms i.e. "where users click, how long they look at a page, where the mouse-cursor hovers, what they type, and more" translate into feelings that are more telling than our curated posts. These emotions provide understanding of ourselves, society, and each other. It is not what we post, but what we don't post and how we actually interact with other posts that provide the most insight. Greene explains that "this 'passive' type of metadata is profoundly embodied in even deeper ways than many of the things we intentionally publish." This concept is very relatable - every time I use social media or technology, I will be reminded that how I am interacting with what I see is more telling than what I actually choose to show the world.
